# Exocortex — Ancient Greek Readers

**22 public-domain Ancient Greek readers** — adapted texts, graded stories and exercise
books from the 19th century — digitised from public scans, OCR'd **twice** and collated by hand
against the print.

## What this is (and is not)

These are **teaching readers**, not critical editions. Most of them *adapt* their sources —
"Herodotus in Attic Greek" means Herodotus rewritten out of Ionic, stories are simplified,
sentences are shortened. **Do not cite these texts as Herodotus, Thucydides or Xenophon** —
cite them as the reader they are. What they are good for is what they were made for:
large amounts of comprehensible Greek at a manageable level.

## How to read the layout

The transcription follows the **printed page line by line**. That is deliberate — it keeps the
digital text aligned with the scan, so any line can be checked against the original. The side
effect is that the structure looks a little raw: sentences break mid-clause at line ends, and
**verse is not marked up as verse** (poems run as ordinary numbered lines). Use the **¶ Flow**
button on any page to reflow the text into continuous paragraphs when you just want to read.

**Line numbers are ours** — a continuous count through each reader — not the printed line or
section numbers of the book. They exist so errors can be reported precisely. Any line can be
linked directly: append `#l123` to a page URL.

## Accuracy — please read

Every text went through two independent OCR passes, automatic token-level alignment, and a
manual review pass over every place where the two passes disagreed. That catches a great deal,
but **these texts certainly still contain errors** — bad scans, broken accents, dropped words,
occasional OCR inventions that both passes agreed on. Treat them as good working copies, not as
authoritative text.

Two conscious editorial decisions:

* **Grave vs acute is not policed.** Where the two passes differ only in `ὶ` vs `ί`
  (barytonesis at a pause and similar), the difference was not chased down. It carries no
  meaning for a reader and is not worth the effort at this scale.
* **The print is the authority.** Where a source prints something unusual — an Ionic form left
  standing in an atticised text, English speaker labels in a Lucian dialogue, inconsistent
  punctuation — it is kept as printed rather than "corrected".

## A note on Lowe, *The Wars of Greece and Persia*

This was digitised from an **older edition whose scan I have since lost**. The text is the same;
the archive.org copy linked from the page is a later printing and is *borrow-only* there, though
the text itself is long out of copyright. The link is given for reference, not as the exact
source of this transcription.

## Reading tools

Each page has: line-by-line or flowing text, line numbers, sepia / light / dark themes, an
EN/UK interface, adjustable type size, focus mode, and a table of contents.

The Greek is marked `lang="grc"`, so the **Alpheios** browser extension works out of the box.
There is also an **α Alpheios** button that loads the embedded Alpheios library on demand —
click any word for dictionary entry, morphology and inflection tables, no extension needed.
(It is loaded only when you ask for it, because it is a heavy library; the choice is remembered.)

## Why this exists

I am building this **for my own learning first**: I need large amounts of readable, level-
appropriate Greek — comprehensible input — and most of it exists only as page images of
19th-century books. Digitising them is what makes that reading practical, and putting the
results online costs nothing extra.

These texts also feed a **larger project on reading Ancient Greek**, which will be presented
later. This repository is the part of it that is useful on its own, so it is public now.

## Related projects

**[Greek Learner Texts Project](https://greek-learner-texts.org/)** — the direct inspiration
for this repository. A collaborative effort (James Tauber, Seumas Macdonald, Fletcher Hardison
and others) producing openly-licensed, annotated Greek texts specifically for **extensive
reading**, with vocabulary lists and linguistic annotation. They got there first and set the
example; this collection is larger in raw volume of graded readers, but theirs is richer in
annotation and is a proper collaborative project. Start there too.

**[Open Greek Corpus](https://github.com/open-greek/open-greek-corpus)** — the same idea applied
to the **originals** rather than to learner adaptations: a free counterpart to the subscription
corpora, combining open digital editions with large-scale OCR of public-domain printed volumes
(~1,125 OCR'd works). If you want Herodotus rather than "Herodotus in Easy Attic Greek", that is
where to look. (Their linguistic data has also been useful to me elsewhere.)

Both are worth supporting. The three of us are digging the same tunnel from different ends:
they cover the original literature and the annotation layer, this repository covers the graded
on-ramp that gets a learner to the point of reading it.
